Frequently Asked Questions

What should I do immediately after being bitten by a dog or cat?

To lower the risk of infection, wash the wound right away for at least ten to fifteen minutes with soap and water. Clean the wound, then cover it with a fresh dressing and an antibacterial. Even for minor bites, get medical attention as soon as possible to see whether you need to take antibiotics or get vaccinated against rabies.

When should I see a doctor after a bite?

It is essential that you promptly seek medical assistance, particularly in cases where the bite is deep, exhibits infection symptoms (such as redness, swelling, or pus), or if the animal was not provoked or has an unclear history of rabies vaccination. Medical practitioners are qualified to determine the likelihood of infection, tetanus, or rabies exposure and can suggest alternative therapies or immunizations against rabies.

Do all bites require rabies shots?

Not all bites need a rabies vaccination. Only in cases when the biting animal is thought to have rabies, is not vaccinated, or cannot be watched for rabies symptoms is rabies vaccine required. The risk is reduced if the animal is a pet with current rabies vaccines and is in good health. However, to find out if you require a rabies vaccination, speak with a physician.

Do I need a tetanus shot after a bite?

If your tetanus vaccination is not up to date (within the last 10 years) or if the wound is particularly deep or dirty, you may need a tetanus shot. Itâ€™s important to inform your doctor about your vaccination history, as tetanus can result from bacteria introduced into the wound.

Should I monitor the animal after the bite?

Yes, you should monitor the animal, especially if the bite occurred from a wild animal or an animal with an unclear vaccination history. If the animal behaves strangely, contact authorities immediately for testing.

Are antibiotics always necessary after a dog or cat bite?

Although they are not always required, deep bites, bites on the hands or face, and wounds that exhibit symptoms of infection are frequently treated with antibiotics. In order to decide whether antibiotics are necessary, your doctor will evaluate the extent of the bite as well as your overall health.

What complications can arise from animal bites?

Infections, cellulitis, abscesses, injury to tendons or nerves (if the bite is deep), and in rare instances, illnesses like rabies or tetanus are among the complications from animal bites. Serious consequences can be avoided with prompt medical attention and proper wound care.

I was bitten by a dog 4 months back. The dog is still alive and healthy. Do I still need to get vaccinated?

If the biting dog or cat is healthy after 10 days of observation, vaccination may not be necessary. However, remember that the 10-day observation period is valid strictly for cats and dogs only, and not for other animals.

Who is legally responsible for dog bite injuries?

Dog owners are responsible for controlling their dogs, which means they must follow the leash laws and dog bite laws for their state and any places they visit. A person who allows their dog to run without a leash, or who owns a dog that attacks another person, may be liable for medical expenses and pain and suffering.

Can any vaccine provide lifelong immunity against rabies?

No, rabies vaccination is needed every time there is a danger of virus exposure. Nevertheless, fewer doses may suffice in case of future bites. Also, the need for Rabies Immunoglobulin (RIG) may be eliminated if a previous complete course of vaccination can be documented.
